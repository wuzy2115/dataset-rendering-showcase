1. Background: How Skeleton Rendering Works

To overlay a robot skeleton on a camera image, we need to project 3D joint positions onto the 2D image plane. This requires three pieces of data from the AGIBot dataset:

1. End-effector pose (state/end/position + state/end/orientation in proprio_stats.h5)
The 3D position (XYZ in meters) and orientation (quaternion XYZW) of each arm's flange (wrist). Shape: [N, 2, 3] for position (left arm [:, 0], right arm [:, 1]), where N is the number of timesteps.

2. Camera extrinsics (head_extrinsic_params_aligned.json)
Per-frame camera pose, consisting of a 3×3 rotation_matrix (R) and a 3D translation_vector (t). Together, R and t describe where the camera is and how it's oriented. The _aligned version provides one entry per video frame (temporally aligned with the proprioception data).

3. Camera intrinsics (head_intrinsic_params.json)
The internal optical parameters: focal lengths (fx, fy) and principal point (ppx, ppy). These describe how the camera lens maps 3D camera-space coordinates to 2D pixel positions. Typical values: fx≈322, fy≈430, ppx≈320, ppy≈244 for the 640×480 head camera.

The projection formula

To project a 3D world point onto the image:

# Step 1: Transform from world frame to camera frame
point_camera = R.T @ (point_world - t)

# Step 2: Perspective projection to pixel coordinates
u = fx * (point_camera.x / point_camera.z) + ppx   # horizontal pixel
v = fy * (point_camera.y / point_camera.z) + ppy   # vertical pixel

For this to work correctly, the 3D point and the camera parameters (R, t) must be expressed in the same coordinate frame. This is where the problems begin.

The robot's kinematic chain

The Unitree G1 humanoid robot has the following structure (simplified):

base_link (hip/pelvis, the root of the robot) → waist joints (pitch + lift, 2 DOF) → body_link2 (torso) → head joints (pan + tilt, 2 DOF) → head_link2 → [HEAD CAMERA] → arm joints (7 DOF each) → arm_end_link (flange) → gripper (CTAG2F120S, ~220–248mm extension)

Key point: the head camera and the arm end-effector are on different branches of the kinematic tree, connected through the torso. They share the waist joints but diverge at the head vs. arm joints.

2. The Problem

When we overlay the rendered skeleton on the actual camera image, the projected bones do not align with the visible robot arms in most episodes. The offset ranges from subtle (a few pixels) to severe (skeleton appears in a completely different part of the image).

According to an official response on HuggingFace (Discussion #28), both state/end/position and camera extrinsics are defined in the same coordinate system. The projection formula above should therefore work directly.

However, in practice we observe two distinct failure modes, suggesting the data may not fully satisfy this assumption.

3. Investigation

What the data actually looks like

We examined the raw data for specific episodes where we could visually verify the rendering quality:

Key observation 1: Camera extrinsics are static within each episode.
Despite being labeled as "per-frame aligned," the translation_vector barely changes across frames (variation < 0.0001m). Even for episodes where the robot moves 0.23m during the recording, the camera extrinsics stay essentially constant. This means the extrinsics are a one-time calibration per recording session, duplicated for each frame — not a dynamically tracked camera pose.

Key observation 2: Extrinsics are per-session, not per-episode.
Within the same task, episodes are grouped into "calibration sessions" that share nearly identical extrinsics. For example, Task 698 (1,993 episodes) has 19 distinct calibration groups. The largest group has 686 episodes with cam_trans≈[0.50, -0.01, 0.98], while a smaller group of 199 episodes has cam_trans≈[0.29, -0.04, 0.88]. Different groups correspond to different physical camera/robot configurations at calibration time.

Key observation 3: Camera height tracks waist configuration.
The camera Z coordinate (height) correlates with the waist lift joint value. Episodes with waist_lift=0.37 show cam_z≈1.18, while waist_lift=0.03 gives cam_z≈0.79. This is physically expected — raising the waist raises the head camera. It also confirms that the extrinsics capture the camera's actual spatial position, including the effect of body configuration.

4. Issue A: Completely Wrong Calibrations

Symptom: The skeleton appears in a completely unrelated part of the image, or is entirely off-screen. The rendering is not just shifted — it bears no spatial relationship to the actual robot arms.

Cause: Some recording sessions have camera extrinsics in a different world frame origin from the rest of the data. The camera translation_vector shows the camera at positions like [1.02, 1.02, 2.07] or [-1.11, 0.48, 2.58], when the robot's head camera should be at roughly [0.5, -0.01, 1.0] relative to the robot base. These calibrations appear to be from a room-scale calibration setup where the origin (calibration board) was placed far from the robot.

How to detect: These episodes stand out because their cam_trans values are far from the per-task majority calibration. For example, Task 428 has 54 calibration groups; most have cam_trans_x ≈ 0.49–0.52, but 3 groups have cam_trans_x < -0.3 (clearly from a different coordinate system).

5. Issue B: Systematic Projection Offset

Symptom: The skeleton shape is roughly correct, but it is shifted from the actual arm position — typically vertically. The overall arm structure (shoulder, elbow, wrist angles) tracks the real motion, but the whole skeleton is displaced.

This affects the majority of episodes, not just a few outliers. It is a widespread data-level issue in the AGIBot World dataset, not a rendering code bug.

Why does this happen?

The root cause is not yet definitively identified, but our investigation points to a mismatch between the coordinate frame assumptions. Several factors contribute:

1. Static calibration vs. dynamic robot configuration
The camera extrinsics are captured once per recording session and stay constant. But the robot's waist and head joints change between sessions and sometimes during a session. If the calibration was performed with the robot in a different body configuration (e.g., different waist lift or head tilt) than the actual episode, the camera's true position during the episode differs from what the extrinsics describe.

2. The head camera moves with body joints, but extrinsics don't update
The head camera sits at the end of the kinematic chain: base → waist → torso → head → camera. When the waist lifts or the head tilts, the camera physically moves. However, the extrinsics remain the same static calibration throughout. If state/end/position is correctly reported in an odometry/world frame (as officially stated), but the extrinsics reflect the camera pose at calibration time only, any movement since calibration introduces a discrepancy.

3. Ambiguity in the coordinate frame of state/end/position
The official documentation does not explicitly state which frame state/end/position is in. An official response confirms it shares a frame with the extrinsics, but Discussion #34 (an unanswered community post) specifically asks whether it's in base_link or world frame, and reports a "significant and consistent offset: the projected points appear noticeably below the actual grippers" when assuming they share a frame.

What we ruled out

Camera intrinsics are NOT the issue (for these episodes). All examined episodes have intrinsic parameters consistent with 640×480 resolution: fx≈322, fy≈430, ppx≈320, ppy≈244. No divide-by-2 correction is needed (though this may apply to other episodes — see Community Reports below).

Robot base position (state/robot/position) is NOT a reliable indicator. We initially hypothesized that episodes with large robot base offsets would have worse rendering. This turned out to be false: Task 351 / Ep 815062 has robot_pos=[-0.99, 0.42, 0] (far from origin) but renders correctly, while Task 773 / Ep 923378 has robot_pos=[0, 0, 0] but has visible offset.

The rendering code itself is correct. The projection formula R.T @ (p - t) matches the official recommendation from the AGIBot team. The URDF skeleton chain computation (from end-effector to arm base link) only involves arm joints, so waist/head joints do not affect the chain geometry. The issue is in the input data, not the code.

6. Community Reports (HuggingFace Discussions)

Multiple users have reported similar issues on the AGIBot World HuggingFace repository. These reports corroborate our findings and provide additional context:

Discussion #28 — Camera extrinsics and hand pose
Official response from the AGIBot team (BobXie): "the head camera's extrinsic and hand poses are defined in the same coordinate system." The recommended projection formula is confirmed as R.T @ (point - t). Despite this, multiple users in the thread report visualization errors.

Discussion #34 — Coordinate systems & transformations for EE-pose projection
A user (KY97) reports: "the projected points appear noticeably below the actual grippers" when using the official projection formula. They ask whether state/end/position is in base_link or world frame, and whether there is a T_base2world transform available. No official response was provided.

Discussion #32 — Camera-to-base conversion script
Users request a URDF file and camera-to-robot-base conversion script. The AGIBot team responds that they "haven't been able to upload the URDF" and that the frame-by-frame extrinsics should make the conversion unnecessary. Users also report inconsistent camera intrinsic parameters across episodes: some episodes have correct intrinsics, while others appear to be calibrated for a different resolution (potentially requiring a divide-by-2 correction).

Discussion #37 — Camera extrinsics reference frame
A user asks whether the extrinsics represent camera-to-world or world-to-camera transform. No official response.

Missing URDF and flange-to-gripper transform:
The AGIBot team has not released the robot's URDF file. Additionally, the fixed transform from the robot flange (where state/end/position is reported) to the gripper tip (where the visible gripper is) has not been provided. The gripper (Changtaik CTAG2F120S) extends 220–248mm beyond the flange, which could account for part of the observed offset between projected skeleton and visible gripper.

8. Current Status & Next Steps

Issue A (wrong calibrations): Detectable and filterable. These episodes should be excluded from training data that requires accurate skeleton rendering. We have identified the affected calibration groups per task.

Issue B (systematic offset): This is a widespread data quality issue in the AGIBot World dataset. The root cause is likely related to the static nature of the calibration and/or ambiguity in the coordinate frame of state/end/position. Possible mitigation strategies:

  • Accept as-is for training: The offset may not significantly impact video generation model training, since the skeleton provides approximate spatial guidance even with some displacement.
  • Attempt FK-based correction: Use the URDF model with waist/head joint angles to compute the expected camera position, then derive a per-frame correction transform. This requires an accurate URDF (not officially released by AGIBot).
  • Wait for upstream fix: The AGIBot team is aware of the community questions about coordinate frames. A future dataset update may clarify or fix the issue.
Note: This analysis was performed on the processed AGIBot Beta dataset (44,358 unique episodes / 93,981 action clips). The findings may not apply identically to the Alpha release, though the same data format and calibration approach are used.
